We classify texts based on their genre: there are mystery novels, thriller novels, romantic novels, tragedies, comedies, epics, etc. Even non-fiction works have genres too: histories, biographies, essays, so on and so forth.

One area of the digital humanities explores genre prediction: whether a computer can guess the genre a text belongs to based on various metrics, such as average word length, "readability" of a text, etc.

In this section, we take 15 texts from 7 genres. 3 are non-fictional: biographies, textbooks and philosophical texts. 4 are fictional: tragedies, science fiction, gothic novels and fantasy novels. For each of these texts, two metrics are computed: the average length of a sentence, as well as the "past tense ratio": the ratio of past tenses and participles to the total number of words. This is by no means a "good" methodology by any standards of research: it is merely an experiment to show how we can predict a text based on literary metrics.
